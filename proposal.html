Observations

Setting: A music festival

A relatively small music festival with only two stages. Most people seem to be under the age of 40, with their friends or family.

In the middle of a concert, a young woman tries to find a path between other festival goers. After making a few steps, she stops, seems to go on her tiptoes and moves her head around, possibly scanning the area for something or someone. After a short while, the person walks away, towards the left of the stage. A few minutes later, the same person comes back, stops again. A few meters away, a second young woman is waving their phone above her head. The first person seems to see the waving phone and starts walking toward it.

Observations to persona: In this scenario, the two festival goers used visual cues to find each other. As this festival was relatively small, text messaging each other’s location and using cues such as waving mobile phones was appropriate. In the case of a larger festival, people easily get separated from their friends and mobile phones are usually of little use when the network becomes too crowded. These observations lead to person 1, Lara, the festival goer.

Setting: On lower field (university campus)

Students walk through the field or along the field toward the gates. Others walk in the opposite direction. Most seem to walk with a purpose.

A small child, at most 5 years old, runs from the rightmost, to the leftmost part of the field. A few seconds later, a woman, which the observer assumes to be the child’s mother appears from the same direction the child came from. She runs toward the child and firmly grabs his arm. They walk off campus, the woman holding the child’s hand.

Observation to persona: In this situation, the woman presumed to be the child’s mother was able to catch her son before he ran too far away from her. However, if the woman had more than one child running away, she might have momentarily lost one or both children. In this case, as the setting was an open space, some safety concerns for the child might arise. However, in closed and safe areas (e.g. toy store, a relative’s house with a large backyard, a children’s museum), the parent might slightly less wary of their child running off to play games. In all of these situations, the parent needs to have a way of finding their children. These observations lead to persona 2, Marcia the mom of two.

Setting: A building on the university campus

Some people walk into the building in groups while others walk in alone. Most are students.

One particular student walks to the elevator with their mobile phone, possibly text messaging someone. While waiting for the elevator, the performer attempts to place a call but the other person does not seem to pick up. The performer gets on the elevator (as does the observer). They then both step off and the performer attempts to place a call again. This time, the other person picks up and the first performer asks where the other person is. After asking more questions about the other person’s location, the performer steps back into the elevator.

Observation to persona: In this situation, the performer looks as though they are struggling to find someone. Although in this particular case, a mobile phone solved the problem, the performer had to (the observer assumes) call the person twice. In a similar but slightly different situation, the performer might have had trouble finding their friend if they were in an unfamiliar building. Similarly, if the person was at a university sanctioned event or a large conference or convention, they might have found the task of finding their friend a lot more complex. In these types of settings, using mobile phones to make calls might be considered as a lack of tact. Alternatively, the person may not have the contact information of the person they want to talk to (e.g. a speaker, an organizer, a volunteer,..). These observations lead to persona 3, JD.

Problem

Locating a person of interest in a confined space can be a challenging task. If you want to interact with a specific person who you know of, but do not know personally, you have no way of locating them.

This problem was observed when one of our group members attended a programming competition. He needed to find one of the mentors who were involved in running the competition to ask a question. He could not find or identify a mentor. Instead he had to walk around aimlessly and ask people questions until he could find one. In this situation, both people are in close proximity being at the same event so some sort of GPS based solution would not have been precise enough to help.

This lead us to imagine other scenarios where a user is trying to locate someone in close proximity. Maybe you want to find a specific person at a conference, a certain employee of a company at a job fair, or a guide at a museum. There is an opportunity for a system to bring the user to the person of interest in order to save time and eliminate the stress of not being able to find someone. If the location is a crowded space, it further increases the need for a solution since it is harder to find people in crowds. Allowing the user to engage in an interaction that will guide them to their person of interest in a relatively close proximity situation could potentially be very useful.

The system could be used to put you in touch with a person with a desired skill set at a job fair. If everyone enabled themselves to be discoverable somehow, you could find people you are interested in and walk up to them and introduce yourself.

Often at music festivals it is not possible to get cellphone service to get in contact with your friends. If a solution was developed that was not entirely reliant on internet connection it could be used in more situations.

Related Products

Since GPS-enabled smartphones have become commonplace, there has been a surge of location-based applications and devices on the market. However, most of them are geared towards street navigation and are not precise enough for close-proximity navigation.

Google Maps

Google Maps is an outdoor mapping application. It is designed for street navigation and also contains navigable maps of other outdoors locations, such as national parks. This is a web service and as such requires a data connection to work. It also requires a GPS signal. This application is a relevant product for outdoor locations where high-quality data and GPS signals are present.

Google Indoor Maps is a new project by the same company. Their aim is to map the indoors of major buildings accross the world. Like Google Mpas, this is a web service and as such requires a data connection to work. It also requires a GPS signal for real-time navigation, which is generally not possible in closed environments. This application is a relevant product for indoor locations that have been mapped through the service, and where high-quality data connections are present and users use landmarks to navigate.

Estimote Indoor Navigation

Estimote is an indoor location service that uses beacons. However, just like Google Indoor Maps, this is meant for indoors landmark navigation; their application is designed to recognized fixed-location beacons. This system is a relevant product for indoor locations where maps for the application are available, and where users use landmarks to navigate.

Apple Find My Friends

Apple's Find My Friends is an application that closely represents our concept. This application allows a user and their friends to mutually on the application. From there, loading the application shows a users' friends on a map and provides directions to their location. This application is relevant since it uses persons as destination for navigation, as opposed to landmarks as for the products mentioned above.

Product Comparison

The key differentiator for our product is that the user does not require prior interaction with the person of interest. For example, both Google solutions would require agreeing with the person of interest in advance on a landmark to navigate to, either indoors (such as the Microsoft booth at a conference) or outdoors (such as the McDonalds near the exit at a festival). The Estimote solution would require similar a similar landmark agreement for indoor navigation. Our solution simply provides directions to the person of interest, avoiding this overhead.

The Google Indoor Maps and Estimote solutions also require that the interior of the building have been mapped in advance, just like the Google Maps solution requires that the location where our users are have been mapped. Our solution provides spatial directions to the person of interest, avoiding dependency on any kind of external data - making this product usable in any location.

The Find My Friends application require prior connection between the person of interest and the user. It also requires maps of the location, whether indoors or outdoors. Our solution does not require any prior interaction other than the person of interest being in possession of the beacon.

Our solution also does not require any kind of data or GPS connection, contrary to the Google and Apple solutions. This allows it to work in any kind of environment, whether indoors or outdoors, and whether cellular or GPS signals are available.

High-Level Design

The application will be written in Java and will run on android phones. A user will create a profile with their name and necessary information. If they wish to be discoverable, they will connect their beacon emitter via iBeacon protocol to their profile. The profile information along with the beacon information will be uploaded to a cloud database. Parse Core is a cloud database service that integrates with android applications. User 2 will be able to access the database, look for a location where they wish to find User 1, and find User 1’s profile. Once User 2 selects User 1’s profile, User 2 can begin detecting for User 1’s beacon since the beacon information is known. As User 2 walks closer to User 1’s beacon the signal strength will increase indicating that User 2 is getting closer to User 1.

Feasibility Justification

There are two components to this project: the beacon held by the person of interest and the device/application used to locate the beacon. Compact wireless beacons using the iBeacon standard are widely available and they can operate within a range that’s large enough for our application. They can also easily communicate with smartphones.

We believe we have the sufficient programming and development experience for this project, as two members of the team have mobile development experience and one has experience in user experience and user interface design. There’s a lot of documentation on how to program applications that interface with iBeacon devices.

As for the locator application, the main challenge would be technical: such a system would be useless if the location information given is not accurate. We are aiming for simplicity on the design, and as such, our first iteration would be a simple compass-like interface that uses the person of interest as its "north".

We estimate that we'd need 4-5 weeks to develop an accurate tracking algorithm, and another 4-5 weeks for user interface design and testing of the application.
